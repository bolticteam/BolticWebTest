Meet 1
Our first robot focused on testing core mechanisms but revealed several performance issues. The shooter used a polycarbonate frame with incorrect belt sizing, high friction, and no angle adjustment, making it difficult to align and shoot consistently. The intake relied on surgical tubing flaps that were slow and lacked enough grip and compression, while the storage system used an inline push-arm design that resulted in slow transfers and no game-piece sorting capability.
Meet 2
For our second meet, we strengthened the robot by switching to a fully metal shooter frame, correcting belt sizes, and reducing friction to improve reliability. The intake was redesigned with a compliant arm and grip wheels, which improved game-piece pickup, although the mechanism still had too many moving parts and inconsistent contact due to a steep ramp. We also tested a full-size transfer dish, which improved handling but required a steeper transfer path and was too large for the robot’s constraints.
Meet 3
Before the third meet, we focused on refining and simplifying the robot. We added an adjustable hood to the shooter for better shot tuning and continued reducing friction to improve consistency. The intake was simplified by removing unnecessary moving parts, creating more reliable and repeatable game-piece pickup. The transfer system was also redesigned with slimmer transfer arms and a more efficient layout, improving compatibility with the rest of the robot.
League Meet
By the league meet, the robot had evolved into a reliable competition-ready design. The shooter featured an adjustable hood, tracking turret, fully metal construction, and optimized belt sizing for accurate and consistent scoring. The intake used a fast, curved ramp with compliant wheels to provide smooth and dependable game-piece collection. Finally, the storage and transfer system incorporated a cut-off dish, less steep transfer ramp, and slimmer transfer arms, resulting in faster, more reliable transfers and improved overall scoring efficiency.
